# Negotiating Vocabularies at Link Time: A Deterministic Six-Class Compatibility Check for Federated Ontology Modules

Dmitry Zharnikov

ORCID: 0009-0000-6893-9231

DOI: [10.5281/zenodo.20751395](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.20751395)

Working Paper v1.0.0 – June 2026

## Abstract

When two research programs use the same word for different concepts, or different words for the same concept, the incompatibility is usually discovered late, by a reader reconstructing it from prose. This paper transfers the link-time compatibility check compilers and package managers apply to code to scholarly vocabularies. If each work declares the terms it owns, imports, and refines as a content-addressed module, a federated linker classifies every cross-author interaction into exactly one of six classes, proposes a typed reconciliation in a standard mapping format, and fails a continuous-integration gate on the three unresolved classes, before either author reads the other's prose. The classification is a deterministic function of two parsed module sets grounded in content-addressed definition-hash identity; it occupies the zero-uncertainty corner of the matching space and adds the reconciliation and gate layer matching omits. The author implements the method as a dependency-light tool and evaluates it on five real, independent vocabulary pairs that exercise the full class set: a clean intra-author federation, an incumbent vocabulary yielding a dangling reference, a cross-disciplinary vocabulary yielding a same-key conflict, a large biomedical ontology yielding dangling references grounded in its own term-obsoletion governance, and a cultural-heritage standard yielding cross-domain reuse at volume.

**Keywords**: ontology matching, link-time compatibility, content-addressed identity, vocabulary reconciliation, SSSOM, federated knowledge graphs, continuous integration, scholarly communication

---

Two independent research programs that share a word rarely share its meaning. When one author writes *signal* to mean a brand's composite emission across perceptual dimensions and another writes *signal* to mean a costly, alterable attribute that conveys hidden quality under information asymmetry, the two vocabularies collide on a bare token whose definitions share no content. The collision is real, it is consequential for anyone trying to integrate the two literatures, and — under current practice — it is discovered only by a human who reads both prose corpora closely enough to notice. The same is true of the opposite failure: two authors who use different words (*perception cloud* and *brand image*) for closely related concepts, leaving the correspondence latent and unstated. Neither failure is surfaced mechanically, named precisely, or assigned a remedy before the prose is read.

Software engineering solved the structural version of this problem decades ago. A linker refuses to produce an executable when two object files make incompatible demands on a shared symbol, and it reports exactly which two disagree [@levine-1999-linkers-and-loaders]. A package manager refuses to resolve a dependency graph when two constraints on a shared package cannot be simultaneously satisfied, and the convention that governs when a version change is compatible is itself a published specification [@preston-werner-2013-semantic-versioning]. In both cases the check runs at *link time* or *install time* — before the program runs, before either component's internals are inspected — and its verdict is deterministic: a given pair of inputs always yields the same answer, and the answer names the precise point of disagreement.

This paper transfers that check to scholarly vocabularies. The proposal is narrow and concrete. Each work declares, as a small content-addressed module, the terms it *owns* (introduces with a definition), *imports* (uses from another module), and *refines* (narrows from another module with an explicit narrowing clause). A federated linker then takes two authors' module sets and classifies every cross-owner interaction into exactly one of six mutually exclusive classes, proposes a typed and justified reconciliation for each, and fails a continuous-integration gate when any interaction falls into one of the three classes whose reconciliation has not yet been authored. Because a term's identity is the cryptographic hash of its definition, "these two authors mean the same thing by this word" and "these two authors mean different things by this word" become mechanical determinations rather than judgment calls.

This paper makes three contributions, each mapped to a named section below.

1. **A six-class taxonomy of cross-owner module interactions** (*The Six Interaction Classes*). Every cross-owner interaction is assigned to exactly one of `AGREEMENT`, `CROSS_IMPORT`, `CROSS_REFINE`, `CONFLICT`, `INCOMPATIBLE_REFINE`, or `DANGLING_IMPORT`, each mapped to a content-addressed identity test, a mapping predicate from a standard vocabulary, a reconciliation operation, and gate semantics that specify which three classes fail the build.

2. **A deterministic reference linker** (*The Reference Implementation*). A dependency-light tool classifies every interaction as a pure function of two parsed module sets and emits the proposed mappings in a standard interchange format — no network, no random seed, no credentials — reusing the exact parser of the single-author linker it generalizes, so the two can never disagree on how a module is read or a definition hashed.

3. **An evaluation on five real, independent vocabulary pairs** (*Evaluation*) that exercises the full class set: a clean intra-author federation, an incumbent brand-theory vocabulary that produces a genuine dangling reference, a cross-disciplinary economic vocabulary that produces a genuine same-key conflict, a large biomedical ontology (the Gene Ontology) that produces dangling references grounded in its own term-obsoletion governance at scale, and a cultural-heritage standard (CIDOC-CRM) that produces clean cross-domain reuse at volume — each with pre-registered hypotheses, falsifiers, and a reproducibility manifest, and an explicit scoping of the one open rung (a living independent co-author).

The method is the empirical companion to a broader infrastructure program that reconceives a research program as a version-controlled repository and a paper as a render of that repository at a point on its timeline [@zharnikov-2026-research-as-repository-git-native]. That program states the federated result in two paragraphs and points here for the mechanism; this paper owns the taxonomy, the tool, and the evaluation in depth, and assumes rather than re-argues the repository reconception.

## Related Work and Positioning

**The matching problem and where this method sits.** Ontology matching is the problem of finding, for two ontologies, an alignment — a set of correspondences between their entities [@euzenat-shvaiko-2013-ontology-matching]. The canonical taxonomy of matching techniques distinguishes string and lexical methods, structural methods, instance-based methods, logic-based methods, and learned or machine-learning methods; the database-side antecedent of the same problem is schema matching, surveyed by Rahm and Bernstein [-@rahm-bernstein-2001-schema-matching-survey]. The standard evaluation campaign for matchers, the Ontology Alignment Evaluation Initiative, has run annual tracks since 2004 and reports precision and recall against reference alignments [@shvaiko-euzenat-2021-oaei-results].

This method deliberately does not compete in that campaign, and a precision/recall score against a reference alignment would be a category error for it. The Ontology Alignment Evaluation Initiative measures, for a system that *generates* candidate correspondences under uncertainty, what fraction of the correspondences it proposes are correct (precision) and what fraction of the true correspondences it recovers (recall) [@shvaiko-euzenat-2021-oaei-results]. This method generates no candidates and scores no correspondences: it does not search a hypothesis space, so it has no false positives to penalize and no recall to fall short of. Every correspondence it emits is forced by a declared interaction — same owned key, import edge, or refine edge — and its identity verdict is byte-equality of a content-addressed definition hash, which is exact by construction; precision and recall are simply not defined on a function that neither proposes nor ranks. It asserts no similarity scores and discovers no candidate correspondences; it classifies *declared* interactions — same key, import edge, refine edge — and decides identity by byte-equality of a content-addressed definition hash. On the Euzenat–Shvaiko axes it is purely terminological and element-level, and it is a *verification* mechanism rather than a *discovery* one. What it adds on top is the layer the matching literature leaves to post-hoc human judgment: an interaction is not merely scored, it is *classified* into one of six named classes; each class carries a *reconciliation operation*; and a continuous-integration *gate* fails the build when the federation is not clean. The output is emitted in the Simple Standard for Sharing Ontological Mappings [@matentzoglu-2022-sssom], using mapping predicates from the Simple Knowledge Organization System [@miles-bechhofer-2009-skos-reference], so the proposals are interoperable with the existing mapping-curation ecosystem.

**Identity and versioning: the changed-definition rule.** That a substantive change to a term's definition is an *evolution* of the ontology rather than a mere schema edit — and so warrants new identity rather than silent mutation — is the long-standing position of the ontology-evolution literature [@noy-klein-2004-ontology-evolution]. The closest prior art for the identity model is the governance practice of the Open Biological and Biomedical Ontologies Foundry, whose principles require that a substantive change to a term's definition warrant a new identifier rather than a silent mutation of the existing one [@smith-2007-obo-foundry; @jackson-2021-obo-foundry-in-2021]. The content-addressed definition hash used here is the automatic, byte-exact form of that policy: identity *is* the hash of the definition, so a definitional change yields a new identity with no human identifier-minting step. The hash construction is the content-addressing idea of Merkle [-@merkle-1988-digital-signature], realized at scale in content-addressed file systems whose object identity is the hash of the object's bytes [@benet-2014-ipfs], and the closest scholarly-artifact analogue is the use of cryptographic-hash-verifiable, immutable identifiers for linked-data artifacts [@kuhn-2014-trusty-uris]. The broader motivation — machine-actionable, mergeable knowledge objects, decentrally published with verifiable provenance [@kuhn-2016-nanopublications] — is the FAIR program's [@wilkinson-2016-fair-guiding-principles], and the modularity framing is the design-rules tradition of decomposing a system into content-addressed parts with declared interfaces [@baldwin-2000-design-rules-power].

**Learned versus deterministic conflict resolution.** A second, more recent body of work detects or learns conflicts rather than preventing them. Continuous and collaborative ontology engineering studies the live reconciliation of evolving ontologies in a development workflow [@salamon-2026-continuous-ontology-engineering]. Benchmarks for inter-context conflicts in retrieval-augmented generation measure how well models detect inconsistencies across retrieved contexts, and report that detection is hard, especially under multi-hop reasoning [@lee-2025-magic-rag-conflict-benchmark]. Surveys of background knowledge and learned techniques in ontology matching map the similarity-and-learning branch this method deliberately defers [@portisch-2023-background-knowledge-matching]. The nearest work in spirit reuses ontology *matching* to drive ontology *versioning* — recasting version detection as a matching task solved with similarity measures and a large-language-model agent [@qiang-2024-om4ov]; this method takes the opposite stance on identity, refusing similarity entirely in favor of byte-equality of a content-addressed definition hash. The precise delta is two-fold. First, those methods are *detective* or *learned*: they identify or predict conflicts, often after the fact, with a confidence attached. This method is *preventive* and *deterministic*: it refuses to link a federation containing an unresolved interaction, with no confidence threshold to tune. Second, this method does not aim to replace learned reconciliation; it is the zero-uncertainty baseline against which learned methods are measured — the point in the space where the answer is a function of declared identity alone. To the author's knowledge, no prior work frames vocabulary alignment as a compile or link-time check, nor transfers a software link-time compatibility check to terminology integration; this is stated defensively, as a gap a grounded literature search did not fill, not as proven non-existence.

## Method

***The Module Schema and Content-Addressed Identity***

A *module* is a content-addressed triple: an owner (the authority that authored it), a set of owned terms, and a set of import and refine relations onto terms owned elsewhere. Each owned term carries a `term_key` (a globally unique, lowercase identifier), a human label, and a definition string. The identity of a term is content-addressed: its `def_hash` is the SHA-256 of the trimmed definition text. Two terms are the same term iff their `def_hash` is byte-identical. This makes "same definition" a mechanical determination and gives the changed-definition rule automatically: any substantive edit to a definition produces a new hash, hence a new identity, with no human identifier-minting.

*Owns, imports, refines.* A module `owns` a term when it introduces it with a definition; exactly one module owns any given `term_key` (the introducing authority). A module `imports` a term to use a term owned by another module by reference. A module `refines` an imported term when it narrows it; a refine must carry an explicit `narrows_to` clause stating the narrowing. The discipline — one owner per term, and a narrowing clause on every refine — is what gives the well-formed-module assumption its force: a violation does not corrupt the classification, it *surfaces as* one of the unresolved classes.

***The Six Interaction Classes***

Given two authors' module sets, every cross-owner interaction falls into exactly one of six classes. Table 1 gives, for each class, its trigger, whether it is resolved, the proposed mapping predicate, and the reconciliation operation. The reconciliation operations — lock, fork, rebase, merge — are the single-author operation vocabulary lifted to act across distinct owners rather than across one author's timeline.

**Table 1.** The Six Cross-Owner Interaction Classes and Their Reconciliation.

| Class | Trigger | Resolved? | Mapping predicate | Reconciliation operation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `AGREEMENT` | both own the key, identical `def_hash` | yes | `skos:exactMatch` | MERGE: assert exactMatch; either may import the other |
| `CROSS_IMPORT` | one imports a term the other owns | yes | `skos:exactMatch` | none — a clean dependency edge |
| `CROSS_REFINE` | one refines the other's term, with a `narrows_to` | yes | `skos:narrowMatch` | REBASE: the refiner's term narrows the owner's |
| `CONFLICT` | both own the key, different `def_hash` | no | `skos:closeMatch` | NAMESPACE the keys + curate; FORK if concepts differ |
| `INCOMPATIBLE_REFINE` | one refines the other's term, no `narrows_to` | no | — | BLOCK until a `narrows_to` is supplied |
| `DANGLING_IMPORT` | one imports/refines a term neither owns | no | — | BLOCK until some author owns it or it is dropped |

*Notes*: "Resolved?" indicates whether the interaction permits a clean federation. The three unresolved classes (`CONFLICT`, `INCOMPATIBLE_REFINE`, `DANGLING_IMPORT`) are the set the continuous-integration gate fails on. Every conflict carries a single `skos:closeMatch` proposal flagged for manual curation; whether the colliding key denotes the same concept under divergent definitions or two distinct concepts is recorded in the proposal's note, not in the predicate, so the conflict class always emits one predicate.

***Reconciliation and Gate Semantics***

Each class is mapped to a mapping justification and confidence for the emitted standard mapping rows: mechanical hash or lexical matches (the resolved classes) carry a lexical-matching justification at high confidence; conflict and cross-refine proposals carry a manual-curation justification at moderate confidence, recording that a human decision is still owed. The continuous-integration gate exits nonzero — fails the build — if and only if any interaction is in one of the three unresolved classes. A passing gate therefore certifies a *linkable* federation: every interaction is an agreement, a clean import, or a compatible refinement.

***Partition and Gate Soundness***

The classification function is total over ordered author pairs and its six classes partition the interaction space. An *interaction* here is a per-edge event: a single shared owned-key, a single import edge, or a single refine edge — not a `term_key`. This distinction makes "exactly one class per interaction" well-defined, because a single `term_key` can legitimately participate in more than one interaction (it may be a shared owned-key *and* the target of an import edge), yielding more than one finding; what the partition claims is that each such finding is uniquely classified, not that each key produces exactly one finding. The tool's loops range over disjoint edge types — owned-key overlap, refine edges, import edges — so any single key participates in at most one owned-key interaction, and each edge is visited by exactly one loop. The argument is a case split on the structure of an interaction, in the proof-sketch style appropriate to a methods paper rather than a fully mechanized proof [@pierce-2002-types-and-programming-languages]. An interaction is induced either by a shared owned key, by an import edge, or by a refine edge. For a shared owned key, the `def_hash` values are either equal (`AGREEMENT`) or unequal (`CONFLICT`) — mutually exclusive and exhaustive. For a refine edge onto a term the other author owns, the `narrows_to` clause is either present (`CROSS_REFINE`) or absent (`INCOMPATIBLE_REFINE`). For an import or refine edge whose target is owned by neither author, the interaction is `DANGLING_IMPORT`; for an import whose target the other author owns, it is `CROSS_IMPORT`. Each interaction matches exactly one antecedent, so the function assigns exactly one class. The gate is sound with respect to the three classes whose reconciliation is not yet authored: it fails precisely when an interaction is `CONFLICT`, `INCOMPATIBLE_REFINE`, or `DANGLING_IMPORT`, so a passing gate implies every interaction is in a resolved class and the federation links. The formal content is intentionally confined to this case split; the method's substance is the taxonomy and the tool, not a heavy model.

***Content-Addressed Identity (Reproducibility)***

Determinism replaces statistical identification. The `def_hash` is a pure function of definition text and the classification is a pure function of two parsed module sets: there is no random component, no sampling, no seed. Re-running the linker on the same inputs reproduces byte-identical output. In lieu of a fixed random seed, each experiment carries an *integrity manifest*: the `def_hash` of every shared term, recomputed at run time and checked equal to the value in the live ontology graph. The manifest is the tamper-evidence anchor — a reader can recompute each hash from the published definition and confirm it matches.

***Relation to the Single-Author Linker***

The federated linker is a strict generalization of a single-author one, not a replacement. The single-author linker enforces one owner per term and *aborts* on a unique-owner collision: within one corpus, two modules claiming the same key is a bug. That is correct when modules are temporal slices of one author's program, reconciled by rebasing the author's later self onto the earlier. Across authors the same collision is not a bug but a negotiation to surface, so the federated linker replaces abort-on-collision with classify-and-propose. Critically, both regimes use the *same parser and the same hash function*: the federated tool imports the single-author linker's module reader and `def_hash` directly, so the two can never disagree on how a module is read. The single-author case is the degenerate federation in which the two authors are one person at two times and the negotiation collapses to a rebase against one's own past self.

## The Reference Implementation

The method is implemented as a single dependency-light tool that reuses the single-author linker's module parser and content-addressing. It loads each author's modules from a directory (the directory name is the author's namespace for qualifying colliding keys), indexes each author into owned terms with their hashes, imports, and refines, classifies every cross-owner interaction by the rules of Table 1, and prints a report grouped by class with the proposed reconciliation per finding. With one flag it also writes the proposed term-to-term mappings as a standard mapping table — emitting a row only for the classes that yield a correspondence (agreement, conflict, cross-refine, cross-import), since a dangling import is a missing-owner gap with no correspondence to assert. With another flag it exits nonzero whenever any interaction is unresolved — the continuous-integration gate. Default behavior reports and exits zero: surfacing incompatibilities is the point, so unlike the single-author linker it never aborts. The tool requires no network, no credentials, and no random seed; its only side effect is the optional mapping file it is asked to write, and it neither reads nor writes the live substrate during a run.

## Evaluation

The evaluation is an existence proof in the case-study style appropriate to a tools-and-systems contribution, not a statistical benchmark. Five real, independent vocabulary pairs were chosen to exercise the full class set across increasing scale and domain distance; each run is a single deterministic, byte-reproducible execution with a pre-registered hypothesis and explicit falsifier and an integrity manifest. The first three pairs establish full class coverage on small, hand-curated vocabularies; the last two extend the resolved and unresolved classes to larger, genuinely cross-domain standards — a large biomedical ontology and a cultural-heritage standard. The vocabularies are inputs to the experiments, not the subject of the paper; the paper argues the mechanism and its class coverage, not the brand, economic, biomedical, or heritage theory the vocabularies encode.

***Case 1: Clean Intra-Author Federation***

The first pair is two convergent programs by one author — a brand-perception vocabulary and an organizational-specification vocabulary built to interoperate. The hypothesis was that the federation is clean by construction and exhibits only resolved classes. The linker reported four cross-imports (the organizational vocabulary importing the perceptual terms `brand-conviction`, `coherence-type`, `cohort`, `observer-spectral-profile` unchanged) and two cross-refines (the organizational vocabulary narrowing `perception-cloud` and `spectral-dimensions` with explicit narrowing clauses, each proposed as a `skos:narrowMatch` to be reconciled by rebasing). Zero interactions were unresolved; the gate passed (exit 0). The result confirms the hypothesis and makes a subtler point: even a federation that is clean by construction still requires explicit cross-refine reconciliation to remain machine-actionable — the narrowing is recorded as a typed edge, not left implicit.

***Case 2: Independent Incumbent, Dangling Import***

The second pair negotiates the brand-perception vocabulary against an independent incumbent: David Aaker's canonical brand-equity and brand-identity model [@aaker-1991-managing-brand-equity; @aaker-1996-building-strong-brands], faithfully transcribed term by term with each definition attributed to its published source. The directional prediction, fixed before the run, was that a genuine independent incumbent produces a real dangling import (it presupposes base primitives no theory-ontology owns) but no same-key conflict, because the perception vocabulary's deliberately qualified naming (it owns `perception-cloud`, not "brand image"; `brand-conviction`, not "brand attitude") forecloses bare-key collisions. The linker produced exactly two dangling imports — `brand` and `consumer`, the primitives the incumbent presupposes but neither ontology owns — and zero conflicts; the gate failed (exit 1), and the mechanically emitted mapping table was header-only, because a dangling import carries no correspondence. This is the first gate-failing adversarial class produced on independently-authored, non-co-designed vocabularies — an incumbent transcribed from published sources rather than built to interoperate.

The conflict null is informative, not a failed experiment: it is evidence that the perception vocabulary's qualified naming *converts* what would otherwise be same-key conflicts into cross-key correspondences. Those genuine cross-key correspondences (for example `perception-cloud` to "brand image", or `cohort` to "market segment") are invisible to the key-based linker by design — it classifies interactions on shared keys and declared edges, not latent cross-key synonymy — and had to be authored as a separate, hand-curated mapping table, every row flagged as manual curation at moderate confidence. That separation is the intended division of labor: the machine layer stays free of false-agreement risk, and genuine semantic reconciliation is pushed to an explicit, justified, human-curated layer.

***Case 3: Cross-Disciplinary Homonym, Conflict***

The third pair is the one the prior two could not produce: a genuine same-key conflict. It negotiates the perception vocabulary against Michael Spence's economic signaling theory [@spence-1973-job-market-signaling], where both programs legitimately own the bare term `signal` with definitions that share no content — the perception program's composite emission across eight perceptual dimensions versus the economic program's costly, alterable attribute that conveys hidden quality under information asymmetry. The hypothesis was that two independent literatures that each own the bare key with different `def_hash` produce a genuine conflict classified as *same label, divergent definition*, carrying a `skos:closeMatch` proposal at moderate confidence flagged for manual curation, with the gate failing. The linker produced exactly that: one conflict on `signal`, plus one dangling import (`labor-market`, an economic primitive neither author owns); the gate failed (exit 1). Cross-disciplinary homonymy is precisely the condition that qualified naming cannot foreclose — neither author can rename away from a term the other independently and legitimately owns — so the conflict class arises honestly rather than by construction.

***Case 4: OBO-Scale Dangling Import Grounded in Term-Obsoletion Governance***

The first three pairs establish full class coverage on small, hand-curated vocabularies; the next two test whether the classes survive larger, genuinely cross-domain standards. The fourth pair negotiates a faithfully transcribed slice of the Gene Ontology — the namesake ontology of the Open Biological and Biomedical Ontologies Foundry [@ashburner-2000-gene-ontology] — against a downstream resource that consumes it by reference. The hypothesis, fixed before the run, was that a downstream resource whose legacy annotations still cite Gene Ontology identifiers the consortium *retired* under the Foundry's changed-definition governance produces a genuine dangling import (the current ontology slice does not own those obsoleted identifiers), while its annotations against the ontology's *current* terms produce clean cross-imports. The directional prediction was two dangling imports and eight cross-imports. The transcribed slice owned twelve current Gene Ontology terms with their real, stable identifiers; the downstream module imported eight of them and additionally imported two identifiers the consortium had obsoleted — `GO:0000005` ("obsolete ribosomal chaperone activity," obsoleted with no active successor because it conflated a gene-product class with a molecular function) and `GO:0006350` ("transcription," obsoleted and replaced by the narrower-defined `GO:0006351`). The linker produced exactly the prediction: two dangling imports on the retired identifiers and eight cross-imports on the current terms; the gate failed (exit 1), and the emitted mapping table carried the eight cross-import rows but no row for the dangling imports. The dangling here is not a manufactured incompatibility — it is the consortium's own term-obsoletion discipline surfaced mechanically. The content-addressed identity model is the automatic, byte-exact form of exactly that governance rule: a term whose definition changes substantively becomes a new identity, so a legacy citation of the old identifier dangles. This is the dangling-import class produced at realistic scale and grounded in an independent ontology's published governance.

***Case 5: Cultural-Heritage Cross-Import at Volume***

The fifth pair tests whether the *resolved* classes — clean cross-author reuse — scale to a genuine, independent, cross-domain standard. It negotiates a faithfully transcribed slice of the CIDOC Conceptual Reference Model, the cultural-heritage reference ontology ratified as an international standard [@doerr-2003-cidoc-crm], against a perception-provenance instrument that builds on it: rather than re-inventing a people-objects-places-events provenance vocabulary, the instrument reuses the model's classes by reference to express the provenance of each captured perception reflection. The hypothesis was that the dominant interaction is clean cross-import at a volume strictly higher than Case 1's four, with the federation clean, plus one cross-refine where the instrument narrows a class to its application reading. The transcribed slice owned thirteen classes — each definition a verbatim quotation of the official scope note — covering actors, persons, objects, places, time-spans, periods, events, activities, and types. The instrument imported twelve of them unchanged, owned two local bridge concepts the model does not provide, and narrowed one class (the production event, restricted to brand-artifact production) with an explicit narrowing clause. The linker produced twelve cross-imports and one cross-refine, zero unresolved interactions; the gate passed (exit 0). Twelve clean cross-imports in one pair is three times the volume of Case 1, and the federation links cleanly: genuine independence does not force an adversarial class, because the instrument *reuses* the standard rather than competing for ownership of its terms. The interaction class is a function of how the second author relates to the first — reuse versus competing ownership — not of independence per se. Case 5 is the existence proof that the taxonomy's resolved classes scale to genuine cross-domain reuse at volume.

***Class Coverage***

Across the five real pairs the linker classified every interaction and terminated, with no interaction left unclassified and no errors on real modules. The pairs cover five of the six classes: cross-import (Cases 1, 4, and 5), cross-refine (Cases 1 and 5), dangling-import (Cases 2, 3, and 4), and conflict (Case 3). The two added pairs extend the existing classes to larger, cross-domain standards — dangling-import grounded in an independent ontology's term-obsoletion governance at scale (Case 4), and cross-import at three times Case 1's volume on a cultural-heritage standard (Case 5) — rather than adding a new class. The sixth class, agreement, requires two authors to own a key with *identical* definitions; it is realized on an engineered two-author fixture in which a shared `cohort` definition hashes identically and is classified `AGREEMENT` (exact-match, merge), alongside a constructed conflict, cross-refine, and dangling import. The remaining class, incompatible-refine, has no real-case instance because the module discipline requires a narrowing clause on every refine — it is a guard the well-formed-module convention forecloses, surfacing only when an author violates the convention. The fixture realizes AGREEMENT; INCOMPATIBLE_REFINE has no real instance because the module schema requires a `narrows_to` on every refine.

## Scope and Boundary Conditions

The method applies only where four conditions hold; outside them it is inapplicable or inferior to human or learned negotiation. Naming each condition, with a real vocabulary that falls outside it, is the honest answer to "when does this not work."

*Machine-readable intensional definitions.* The content-addressed identity test presupposes that each owned term carries a written definition from which a hash is computed. A vocabulary distributed as a bare list of labels with no definitions — many controlled keyword lists and folksonomy tag sets — has nothing to hash; the method cannot distinguish agreement from conflict on it and does not apply.

*Interaction classification, not cross-key discovery.* The method classifies declared interactions on shared keys and import or refine edges. It does not detect that two *differently keyed* terms mean the same thing. Recovering such cross-key synonymy — the substantive `perception-cloud`-to-"brand image" correspondence of Case 2 — requires an embedding-assisted or human-curated layer, which is deliberately out of scope. A reviewer comparing two vocabularies that share *no* keys but much meaning is outside this method's reach.

*A discipline of owners.* The classification presupposes that each term has exactly one owning authority and that imports and refines are honestly declared. Purely procedural or free-text scholarly artifacts — a methods narrative, a discursive review — have no module structure to read and fall outside the method until they are first given one.

*A continuous-integration culture.* The reconciliation operations and the fail-the-build gate presuppose an ecosystem that runs a gate over its vocabulary and is willing to act on a failing build. In domains where fork and rebase semantics would damage cumulative reference — legal or regulatory ontologies whose identifiers must never re-point — the gate is the wrong instrument even though the classification still applies; there, a conflict is information to record, not a build to fail.

Finally, a falsifier for the general claim, beyond the per-case falsifiers above: if more than a small fraction of real, well-formed scholarly vocabulary pairs encountered in the wild contained a cross-owner interaction that the six classes could not uniquely classify — an unclassifiable seventh class — the taxonomy would be falsified.

## Threats to Validity

*Transcription, not living co-authorship (the principal limitation).* All five real module sets are author-transcribed: the perception author transcribed the incumbent Aaker, cross-disciplinary Spence, biomedical Gene Ontology, and cultural-heritage CIDOC-CRM vocabularies from published sources, and each conflict is resolved only as a tool-proposed curation. The independence is in each vocabulary's provenance — a different author, a different discipline, decades earlier, not built to be compatible — not yet in a living co-author's authoring act. A transcriber's framing choices (which primitives to import, how to word a definition) remain the transcriber's. The paper therefore claims the mechanism and its coverage of the class set on real vocabularies, not multi-author adoption as a validated social practice. The effort to transcribe each incumbent module was modest — on the order of a dozen attributed term definitions per vocabulary — and an automated path exists: extracting candidate definitions from published prose, then hand-confirming, would remove the transcription bottleneck and is named here as future work.

*Five cases are an existence proof, not a robustness benchmark.* Five hand-curated pairs demonstrate full class coverage on real vocabularies, and the two added pairs extend the classes to larger cross-domain standards — a large biomedical ontology (the Gene Ontology) exercising dangling imports at scale and grounded in term-obsoletion governance, and a cultural-heritage standard (CIDOC-CRM) exercising cross-import at three times Case 1's volume. They still do not stress-test the method on full-ontology runs: each pair transcribes a representative slice (twelve to thirteen terms), not the thousands of terms a complete ontology carries. The lead external-validity threat — that full-scale runs and transcription bias undercut generalization — remains real. The runtime is linear in the number of terms (the classification is a set intersection over keys), so scaling is an empirical, not algorithmic, question; a full-ontology run and automated definition extraction to remove the transcription bottleneck are named as future work.

*The conflict null is contingent.* The absence of a same-key conflict in Case 2 is evidence about *this* vocabulary's naming discipline, not a general claim that no incumbent vocabulary can conflict with a perception vocabulary. A vocabulary that owned a bare `brand-image` or `positioning` key *would* conflict; the null is "qualified naming avoids the collision," not "no collision is possible."

## Discussion

*Theoretical implications.* The contribution is a *method*, instantiated in a tool, not a new matcher and not a new theory. Its theoretical weight is the demonstration that a useful and previously unoccupied point exists in the ontology-matching space: the deterministic, content-addressed, link-time vertex, where the answer is a function of declared identity alone and the value added is classification plus reconciliation plus a gate. Three failures that the matching literature leaves dangling become preventable link-time errors under this frame: an incumbent vocabulary's repeated dangling imports against newer ontologies; a cross-disciplinary homonym's conflict against a formalized sense of the same word; and the observation that even clean intra-author federation requires explicit cross-refine reconciliation to stay machine-actionable.

*Practical implications.* For an author or an editor, the practical claim is that terminological compatibility can be checked the way code compatibility is checked: as a gate in continuous integration, run before a human reads the other party's prose, reporting exactly where two vocabularies agree, conflict, or extend one another, and emitting the proposals in a standard interchange format that the existing mapping-curation tooling can ingest. The method does not decide the semantic questions for the author — conflict proposals are flagged for manual curation precisely because a human decision is owed — but it removes the labor of *finding* the incompatibilities and *naming* them.

*What the method adds over a schema validator.* The most contestable reading is that the tool is a schema validator with extra vocabulary — that once two modules are parsed and their `def_hash` values compared, the rest is bookkeeping. The YAML parse and the hash comparison are indeed the easy part; they are not the contribution. The contribution is the layer above the parse: a *closed, named classification* of every cross-owner interaction (a schema validator reports that two definitions differ; it does not say that the difference is a `CONFLICT` as opposed to a `CROSS_REFINE` or a `DANGLING_IMPORT`), and the *operational semantics* attached to each class — a typed reconciliation operation drawn from the lock/fork/rebase/merge vocabulary, a standard mapping predicate emitted into SSSOM, and the continuous-integration gate that fails the build on exactly the three classes whose reconciliation a human still owes. A schema validator answers "is this document well-formed?"; this method answers "given two authors' well-formed modules, what is the *negotiated relationship* between every shared or cross-referenced term, what operation reconciles it, and may the federation link yet?" The novelty is that mapping from declared interaction to reconciliation-operation-plus-gate-semantics, not the parse that precedes it.

*Convergence with other evidence.* The result converges with the changed-definition governance practice of large ontology ecosystems [@smith-2007-obo-foundry; @jackson-2021-obo-foundry-in-2021] and with the content-addressing tradition for verifiable scholarly artifacts [@kuhn-2014-trusty-uris], strengthening rather than threatening the identity model: the method automates, byte-exactly, a rule those communities already endorse. Against the detective and learned conflict-resolution work [@salamon-2026-continuous-ontology-engineering; @lee-2025-magic-rag-conflict-benchmark; @portisch-2023-background-knowledge-matching], it is the preventive, deterministic baseline rather than a competitor.

*Limitations.* The limitations are stated above and bounded: author-transcription rather than living co-authorship (affects the social-adoption claim, not the mechanism), five representative-slice cases rather than a full-ontology benchmark (affects scale generalization, not class coverage), and a contingent conflict null (affects the naming-discipline claim, not the conflict detection).

*Future research.* The open rung is a living, genuinely independent co-author who authors their own modules in this schema and resolves a genuine same-key conflict in dialogue. Beyond it: automated definition extraction to remove the transcription bottleneck and enable a full-ontology run (the two scale-and-volume pairs added here transcribe representative slices, not whole ontologies); and an embedding-assisted layer to propose cross-key synonymy candidates for human confirmation, closing the one correspondence type the key-based linker cannot reach.

## Conclusion

Two vocabularies that share a word rarely share its meaning, and the incompatibility is usually found late, in prose, by hand. This paper transferred the link-time compatibility check of compilers and package managers to scholarly vocabularies: declare each work's terms as a content-addressed module, classify every cross-author interaction into one of six classes, propose a typed reconciliation, and fail a continuous-integration gate on the three unresolved classes — all before either author reads the other's prose. The classification is deterministic and grounded in content-addressed definition-hash identity, and it adds the classification, reconciliation, and gate layer that matching leaves to judgment. On five real, independent vocabulary pairs the method exercised the full class set, producing a clean federation, a genuine dangling reference, a genuine same-key conflict, dangling references grounded in a large biomedical ontology's own term-obsoletion governance, and clean cross-domain reuse at volume against a cultural-heritage standard, each deterministically and reproducibly. The mechanism is established; the remaining work is to run it between living co-authors at scale.
